01 — Biology

Why We Sleep: Memory, Repair, and the Brain's Cleanup Crew

Sleep is not rest — it is active maintenance. During deep sleep, your brain replays the day's experiences and transfers short-term memories into long-term storage. This process, called memory consolidation, is why studying before bed works better than pulling an all-nighter.

Your brain also shrinks slightly during sleep, opening gaps between cells so cerebrospinal fluid can flush out waste proteins — including amyloid-beta, which builds up in Alzheimer's disease. Missing even one night of sleep causes measurable accumulation of these toxic proteins.

Sleep stages matter: REM sleep (when you dream) strengthens emotional memories and creative thinking. Deep slow-wave sleep handles physical repair and immune function. Adults need roughly 90-minute cycles of both, repeated 4–5 times per night.
02 — Technology

How Wi-Fi Works: Radio Waves, Channels, and Congestion

Wi-Fi transmits data using radio waves at either 2.4 GHz or 5 GHz frequencies. Your router converts internet data into radio signals; your device's antenna picks them up and converts them back. The whole process happens millions of times per second.

The 2.4 GHz band travels further and passes through walls more easily, but it's crowded — microwaves, baby monitors, and neighbours' routers all compete on the same frequency. The 5 GHz band is faster and less congested but only works well within about 10 metres of the router.

Why your Wi-Fi slows down at night: Wi-Fi channels are shared. When everyone in your building streams video simultaneously, all devices compete for the same radio bandwidth — exactly like too many people talking in one room.

03 — Medicine

How Vaccines Train Your Immune System

Your immune system learns by experience. The first time it encounters a pathogen, the response is slow — it can take days to build the right antibodies. Vaccines work by showing your immune system a harmless version of a threat so it can prepare its defences in advance.

mRNA vaccines (like the COVID-19 shots) take a different approach. Instead of delivering a piece of the virus itself, they deliver genetic instructions that tell your own cells to briefly produce a harmless protein. Your immune system sees this protein, mounts a response, and builds memory — all without any live virus involved.

Why annual flu shots exist: The influenza virus mutates its surface proteins every year. Scientists monitor which strains are circulating globally and update the vaccine each season — it's a race between viral evolution and immunology.
04 — Engineering

EV Batteries: How Lithium-Ion Cells Store and Release Energy

An electric vehicle battery is a large pack of lithium-ion cells — the same chemistry as your phone battery, just much bigger. Inside each cell, lithium ions move between two electrodes through a liquid electrolyte. When charging, ions move one way; when discharging to power the motor, they move back.

Heat is the main enemy of battery life. Charging too fast generates heat that degrades the electrodes over time. This is why EVs have liquid cooling systems around the battery pack, and why fast chargers slow down automatically once the battery gets warm.

Solid-state batteries: The next generation replaces the liquid electrolyte with a solid material. This allows faster charging, higher energy density, and eliminates the fire risk from liquid electrolyte — but manufacturing at scale remains the key challenge.
05 — Physics

How GPS Knows Exactly Where You Are

GPS works by measuring time. At least 24 satellites orbit Earth, each broadcasting a precise timestamp. Your phone receives signals from several satellites simultaneously and calculates how long each signal took to arrive. Since radio signals travel at the speed of light, the travel time reveals the distance to each satellite.

With distances from three satellites, your position can be narrowed to a circle. A fourth satellite eliminates ambiguity and gives you a precise 3D location. The whole calculation happens in under a second, thousands of times per day.

Relativity correction: GPS satellites move fast and sit in weaker gravity than Earth's surface. Both effects cause their clocks to drift — by about 38 microseconds per day. Without constant relativistic corrections, GPS would accumulate errors of roughly 10 km per day.
06 — Computing

How Social Media Algorithms Decide What You See

Social media feeds are not chronological — they are ranked by machine learning models trained to maximise engagement. Every action you take (like, share, pause, scroll past) is recorded and used to build a detailed model of your preferences. The algorithm then predicts which content will keep you on the platform longest.

These systems optimise for engagement, not accuracy or wellbeing. Content that triggers strong emotions — outrage, excitement, fear — tends to generate more interaction, so the algorithm learns to surface it more often regardless of its quality or truth.

Filter bubbles: When an algorithm only shows you content you already agree with, it gradually narrows your information diet. Research shows this increases political polarisation and reduces exposure to opposing viewpoints — an unintended side effect of pure engagement optimisation.
07 — Chemistry

How Sunscreen Protects Your Skin from UV Damage

UV radiation comes in two main types: UVA (ages skin, penetrates deep) and UVB (causes sunburn, triggers skin cancer). Sunscreen works through two mechanisms — chemical filters absorb UV energy and release it as heat; mineral filters (zinc oxide, titanium dioxide) physically scatter and reflect UV rays.

SPF only measures UVB protection. SPF 30 blocks about 97% of UVB rays; SPF 50 blocks 98%. The difference sounds small but matters for people with fair skin or prolonged sun exposure. "Broad spectrum" on the label means the product also protects against UVA.

Why reapplication matters: Chemical UV filters break down when exposed to sunlight — they absorb UV energy and degrade in the process. Most sunscreens lose significant effectiveness after 2 hours of sun exposure, regardless of their SPF rating.
08 — Microbiology

Antibiotic Resistance: How Bacteria Learn to Survive Our Medicines

Antibiotics kill bacteria by targeting specific biological processes — disrupting their cell walls, blocking protein synthesis, or interfering with DNA replication. But bacteria reproduce rapidly, and random mutations occasionally produce variants that can withstand these attacks. When antibiotics kill the susceptible bacteria, resistant ones survive and multiply.

The problem is accelerated by overuse. Taking antibiotics for viral infections (colds, flu) does nothing useful but still kills susceptible gut bacteria, leaving more room for resistant strains. Incomplete courses are equally problematic — stopping early selects for the hardiest bacteria.

The pipeline problem: Developing a new antibiotic takes 10–15 years and costs over $1 billion. Because patients only use them briefly, the financial return is low — so pharmaceutical companies have largely abandoned antibiotic research, even as resistance grows.
